## The Core Principle: Batch, Don't Bounce

### What is Batching?

**Batching** means doing similar tasks together in one focused time block instead of spreading them throughout the day.

**Example:**

- **Bouncing:** Check email 15 times today, respond one at a time
- **Batching:** Check email 3 times today (morning, lunch, end of day), respond to all at once

### Why Batching Works

Every time you switch tasks, your brain needs time to refocus. This is called "switching cost," and it can waste 20-40% of your productive time.

When you batch tasks:

- You enter a "flow state" faster
- You work more efficiently (muscle memory kicks in)
- You finish tasks in less total time

> **Key Insight:** It's not about working more. It's about grouping your work strategically so you waste less mental energy switching between tasks.

## The Weekly Batching Template

Use this framework to batch your most common teaching tasks:

### Monday: PLANNING POWER HOUR

**Time Block:** 1 hour after school  
**Tasks:**

- Review the week's lessons
- Pull/prep materials for the week
- Identify any gaps or changes needed

**Why Monday?**  
You're fresh from the weekend, and planning for the full week prevents daily scrambling.

**Batching Tip:**  
Don't plan lessons day-by-day. Plan Monday-Friday in one sitting.

---

### Tuesday: COMMUNICATION CATCH-UP

**Time Block:** 30 minutes after school  
**Tasks:**

- Process all parent emails from Monday-Tuesday
- Send any positive updates you've been meaning to send
- Update your class newsletter or communication platform

**Why Tuesday?**  
You've had time to observe students at the start of the week, so you have fresh material to share with families.

**Batching Tip:**  
Use templates (see "Parent Communication Toolkit") to speed up email responses.

---

### Wednesday: GRADING SPRINT

**Time Block:** 45-60 minutes  
**Tasks:**

- Grade all quizzes, homework, or short assignments from the week so far
- Enter grades into gradebook
- Identify students who need follow-up

**Why Wednesday?**  
Mid-week check-in prevents end-of-week panic. You catch problems early.

**Batching Tip:**  
Grade one assignment type at a time (all math quizzes, then all reading responses).

---

### Thursday: ASSESSMENT PREP

**Time Block:** 30 minutes  
**Tasks:**

- Create or finalize assessments for next week
- Print/prep materials
- Prep answer keys or rubrics

**Why Thursday?**  
You have time to adjust if something doesn't work. Also avoids Sunday night stress.

**Batching Tip:**  
Reuse assessments from last year with minor tweaks (don't reinvent the wheel).

---

### Friday: WEEKLY WRAP & NEXT WEEK PREVIEW

**Time Block:** 30 minutes after school  
**Tasks:**

- Finalize grades for the week
- Send home any student work or communication
- Do a quick "brain dump" of what worked / what didn't
- Preview next week's plans (are you ready?)

**Why Friday?**  
End the week with a clean slate. Walk away knowing Monday is prepped.

**Batching Tip:**  
Keep a running "wins and adjustments" doc so you can improve over time.

## Task-Specific Batching Strategies

### 1. Email Batching (Inbox Zero for Teachers)

**The Problem:** You check email 20 times a day, and it derails your focus every time.

**The Solution:** Check email 3 times per day only.

**Batching Schedule:**

- **Morning (7:30 AM):** Scan for urgent issues only. Respond to 1-2 critical emails.
- **Lunch (12:00 PM):** Process all new emails. Respond, delegate, or delete.
- **End of Day (3:30 PM):** Final sweep. Archive everything processed. Leave inbox empty.

**Inbox Zero Rules:**

1. **Delete** (spam, irrelevant)
2. **Delegate** (forward to admin, counselor, etc.)
3. **Defer** (move to "To-Do" folder if it takes more than 5 minutes)
4. **Do** (respond if it takes under 2 minutes)

**Pro Tip:** Turn off email notifications. Close your inbox tab except during your 3 check-in times.

---

### 2. Grading Batching

**The Problem:** Papers trickle in, and you grade them one at a time whenever you have 5 minutes. It takes forever and feels never-ending.

**The Solution:** Collect all work on the same day. Grade all at once using a rubric or comment bank.

**Batching Strategy:**

- **Step 1:** Grade all of one question/section at a time (not one full paper at a time).
  - Why? You get faster because you're looking for the same thing repeatedly.
- **Step 2:** Use a rubric or comment bank (see "Comment Bank Builder") to speed up feedback.
- **Step 3:** Set a timer. Aim for 2-3 minutes per paper for short assignments.

**Examples:**

- Grade all "Question 1" responses across 30 papers, then move to Question 2.
- Grade all thesis statements first, then all body paragraphs, then conclusions.

**Pro Tip:** Use colored pens for different criteria. Green = strengths, yellow = next steps. Saves time writing the same comments.

---

### 3. Lesson Planning Batching

**The Problem:** You plan lessons the night before (or morning of), which means you're always behind.

**The Solution:** Plan a full unit (2-4 weeks) at once, then review weekly to adjust.

**Batching Strategy:**

- **Phase 1 (Start of Unit):** Map out the full unit arc.
  - Learning objectives for each lesson
  - Assessments (formative + summative)
  - Key activities and materials needed
- **Phase 2 (Weekly Review):** Spend 30-60 minutes adjusting based on student progress.
  - What needs reteaching?
  - What can we skip or speed up?

**Pro Tip:** Use the "Flexible Lesson Plan Designer" templates. Plug your content into a proven structure instead of starting from scratch.

---

### 4. Parent Communication Batching

**The Problem:** You respond to parent emails reactively, one at a time, and it drains you.

**The Solution:** Batch all parent communication into one 30-minute block per week.

**Batching Strategy:**

- **Monday/Tuesday:** Collect notes on students (observations, wins, concerns)
- **Wednesday:** Draft all emails in one sitting using templates
- **Thursday:** Review and send

**Types to Batch:**

- Positive updates (aim for 3-5 per week)
- Missing assignment follow-ups (send to all at once)
- Conference requests (batch-send to families who need check-ins)

**Pro Tip:** Use a "parent communication tracker" (simple spreadsheet) to ensure you're contacting every family at least once per term.

## Automation Ideas: Let Technology Work for You

You don't need to be a tech expert to automate repetitive tasks. Here are 10 simple automations:

### 1. Auto-Grading with Google Forms

**What it does:** Students take a quiz in Google Forms, and it grades itself instantly.

**How to set it up:**

1. Create a quiz in Google Forms
2. Mark correct answers in the "Answer Key" section
3. Students get instant feedback; you get a gradebook

**Best for:** Multiple choice, true/false, short answer (with exact match)

---

### 2. Email Templates (Canned Responses)

**What it does:** Save frequently used emails as templates you can insert in 2 clicks.

**How to set it up (Gmail):**

1. Settings > Advanced > Enable "Templates"
2. Compose your email
3. Click three dots > Templates > Save as template

**Best for:** Positive updates, absence follow-ups, conference requests

---

### 3. Assignment Reminders with Google Calendar

**What it does:** Auto-send reminders to students before assignments are due.

**How to set it up:**

1. Create a Google Calendar for your class
2. Add assignment due dates as events
3. Set automatic email reminders (1 day before, 1 hour before)

**Best for:** Reducing "I forgot!" excuses

---

### 4. Attendance Tracking with Google Sheets + QR Codes

**What it does:** Students scan a QR code to mark attendance; data auto-populates in a spreadsheet.

**How to set it up:**

1. Create a Google Form with "Name" and "Date" fields
2. Generate a QR code for the form (use qr-code-generator.com)
3. Post the QR code. Students scan daily.

**Best for:** Fast, contactless attendance

---

### 5. Weekly Newsletter Automation

**What it does:** Pre-write and auto-send your weekly parent newsletter.

**How to set it up:**

1. Use a tool like Smore or Mailchimp
2. Set up a weekly template with sections (upcoming events, homework, celebrations)
3. Fill it in once per week and schedule it to send Friday afternoons

**Best for:** Consistent parent communication without forgetting

---

### 6. Auto-Save Student Work to Google Drive

**What it does:** Students submit work via Google Classroom or Forms, and it auto-organizes by student in your Drive.

**How to set it up:**

1. Create assignments in Google Classroom
2. All student work auto-saves to a folder by assignment

**Best for:** Keeping student work organized without manual filing

---

### 7. Timer for Transitions

**What it does:** Visual timer projects on your screen so students know how much time is left.

**How to set it up:**

1. Use a free tool like ClassroomScreen.com or Online-Stopwatch.com
2. Set the timer and project it during work time or transitions

**Best for:** Reducing "How much time do we have?" questions

---

### 8. Rubric Auto-Fill in Google Docs

**What it does:** Insert rubric feedback into student docs with one click.

**How to set it up:**

1. Create a rubric in a Google Doc
2. Use Text Blaze or Snippets extension to save common feedback phrases
3. Type a shortcut (e.g., ".thesis") and it expands to full feedback

**Best for:** Faster, consistent feedback on writing

---

### 9. Behavior Tracking with Digital Forms

**What it does:** Log behavior incidents quickly on your phone or computer; data auto-organizes in a spreadsheet.

**How to set it up:**

1. Create a Google Form with fields: Student Name, Date, Behavior, Action Taken
2. Bookmark the form on your phone
3. Fill it out in 30 seconds whenever an incident happens

**Best for:** Documentation without carrying a paper log

---

### 10. Scheduled Social Media Posts (for Class Updates)

**What it does:** Pre-write class updates and schedule them to post automatically.

**How to set it up:**

1. Use free tools like Buffer or Later
2. Write your updates for the week (celebrations, reminders, photos)
3. Schedule them to post at optimal times (e.g., 7 PM when parents are online)

**Best for:** Consistent communication without daily posting

## The Inbox Zero System for Teachers

### Step 1: Set Up Email Folders

Create these 4 folders:

- **To-Do:** Emails that need action (but not right now)
- **Waiting:** Emails where you're waiting for a response
- **Archive:** Processed emails you might need later
- **Templates:** Pre-written email templates

### Step 2: Process Email 3x/Day

**Morning Check (5 minutes):**

- Scan for emergencies only
- Move urgent items to "To-Do"

**Lunch Check (15 minutes):**

- Process all new emails using the 4 Ds: Delete, Delegate, Defer, Do
- Move processed emails to Archive

**End-of-Day Check (10 minutes):**

- Final sweep
- Archive everything
- Your inbox should be EMPTY

### Step 3: Weekly Email Purge

Every Friday, review your "To-Do" and "Waiting" folders. Anything sitting there for more than a week either gets done or deleted.

## Batching Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Batching Too Many Things at Once

Don't try to implement all of these strategies at once. Start with ONE.

**Fix:** Pick your biggest time drain (email? grading?) and batch that first.

---

### Mistake 2: Not Protecting Your Batch Time

If you schedule a grading block but then respond to emails during it, you're not batching - you're multitasking.

**Fix:** Close email. Silence phone. Protect your batch time like you'd protect class time.

---

### Mistake 3: Making Batching Too Rigid

Life happens. Students need you. Parents email emergencies. Don't become inflexible.

**Fix:** Build in "flex time" each week for the unexpected.

## FAQ

**Q: What if I don't have time for a full hour of grading?**  
Batch in smaller chunks. Even 15 minutes of focused grading beats 15 minutes of distracted, scattered work.

**Q: Can I batch during the school day?**  
Yes! Use planning periods, lunch, or even 10 minutes before/after class. The key is protecting that time.

**Q: What if I get urgent emails during my non-email times?**  
True emergencies are rare. Most things can wait 2-3 hours. If it's truly urgent, people will call or find you in person.

**Q: Will parents be upset if I don't respond to emails immediately?**  
Set expectations. In your start-of-year email, say: "I check email 3 times daily and respond within 24 hours on weekdays."

**Q: How do I stop feeling guilty about not working all the time?**  
Batching and automation help you work MORE efficiently, not less. You're not being lazy - you're being strategic.
