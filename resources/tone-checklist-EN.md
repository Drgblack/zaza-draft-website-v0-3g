## Before/After Email Examples

### Example 1: Missing Homework

**BEFORE (Harsh Tone):**
> Subject: Alex's homework problem
>
> Alex never does his homework. He's falling behind and you need to make sure he completes it every night. This is unacceptable and if it continues, he will fail.

**AFTER (Professional Tone):**
> Subject: Let's partner on Alex's homework completion
>
> Hi Mr. and Mrs. Johnson,
>
> I'm reaching out because Alex has not turned in 4 of 6 homework assignments over the past two weeks. I know he's capable of the work, as he participates well in class.
>
> To support Alex, could we work together on these steps?  
> 1. At home: Help Alex set aside 20 minutes after dinner for homework.  
> 2. At school: I'll check in with him at the start of each class to confirm he understands the assignment.
>
> Would you be available for a brief phone call this week to discuss?
>
> Thank you,  
> Ms. Thompson

**What changed:**
- Specific details (4 of 6 assignments, 2 weeks)
- Acknowledged a strength (participates well)
- Collaborative tone (work together)
- Clear action steps

---

### Example 2: Behavior Incident

**BEFORE (Defensive Tone):**
> Subject: Jamie's behavior
>
> Jamie was completely out of control today. He refused to follow directions and was disrespectful to me in front of the class. You need to talk to him about his attitude.

**AFTER (Objective Tone):**
> Subject: Checking in after today's class
>
> Dear Ms. Rodriguez,
>
> I wanted to let you know about an incident that happened during class today.
>
> During independent work time, Jamie left his seat repeatedly, talked loudly to peers, and when I asked him to return to his seat, he said, "No, this is boring." This made it difficult for him and others to focus on learning.
>
> I spoke with Jamie privately after class and we agreed he will use our reset spot if he feels frustrated. I'm confident this was a one-time situation, but wanted to keep you informed.
>
> Please let me know if Jamie shared anything with you about today or if there's context I should know.
>
> Best,  
> Mr. Davis

**What changed:**
- Objective description (what happened, not character judgment)
- Specific quotes and timing
- What you did in response
- Invitation for parent input

## Emergency Tone Fixes

### What if you already sent an email you regret?

**Option 1: The Follow-Up Clarification**

If your tone was too harsh or unclear, send a brief follow-up:

> Hi [Parent Name],
>
> I wanted to follow up on my last email. I realize my tone may have come across as [harsh/frustrated], and that wasn't my intention. What I meant to say is: [clarify your message in softer language].
>
> I care about [Student Name] and want us to work together. Could we set up a time to talk by phone?
>
> Thank you for your understanding.
>
> Best,  
> [Your Name]

---

**Option 2: Request a Phone Call**

Sometimes email isn't the right medium. If things are escalating:

> Hi [Parent Name],
>
> I realize email isn't the best way to discuss this. Could we set up a time to talk by phone this week? I'd like to hear your perspective and work together on a plan for [Student Name].
>
> Best,  
> [Your Name]

---

**Option 3: Loop in Admin (If Necessary)**

If the parent responds angrily or things are escalating, stop responding. Forward the thread to your administrator and request their guidance before replying.

## Bonus: The "Sleep on It" Rule

If you're writing an email while angry, frustrated, or upset:

1. **Write the email**
2. **Save it as a draft** (do NOT send)
3. **Walk away for at least 2 hours** (or sleep on it)
4. **Reread it** with fresh eyes
5. **Run it through the 5-Minute Tone Audit**
6. **Revise as needed**
7. **Then send**

> **Why this works:** Your emotional state affects your word choice. A 2-hour buffer gives you perspective.

## FAQ

**Q: How do I know if my tone is too casual or too formal?**  
Match the parent's tone. If they email casually ("Hey! Thanks!"), you can be warmer. If they're formal ("Dear Mr. Smith"), mirror that.

**Q: What if I'm writing about a sensitive topic (divorce, custody, poverty)?**  
Be empathetic and avoid assumptions. Example: "I wanted to reach out to see if there's any support [Student Name] might need during this transition."

**Q: Can I use emojis in parent emails?**  
Only if the parent uses them first AND the context is appropriate (positive updates, not serious concerns).

**Q: What if a parent doesn't respond to my email?**  
Follow up once after 3-5 days. If still no response, document the attempt and follow your school's protocol.

**Q: How do I handle emails when I'm angry?**  
Don't send it. Save as draft, walk away, and apply the "Sleep on It" rule.
