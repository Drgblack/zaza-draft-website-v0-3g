# Zaza Draft X Content System

Purpose: turn teacher pain posts on X into helpful, low-pressure engagement that earns trust, drives clicks to `zazadraft.com`, and supports the pain-first landing pages already live on the site.

Audience: tired teachers, form tutors, heads of year, classroom teachers, pastoral leads, and report-weary school staff.

Voice: empathetic colleague. Calm, specific, useful. Never salesy. Never smug. Never "AI will fix everything". Zaza is the co-writer that helps teachers sound professional and appropriate when the wording feels risky.

Core rule: most posts should help first. Around 20 to 30 percent of threads should end with a soft trial link. The rest should link to a useful landing page or blog page when there is a clear fit.

## Post Framework

Use this structure for almost every post:

1. Open with a teacher moment that feels real.
2. Name the emotional problem quickly.
3. Give a calm, practical structure or script.
4. End with one low-pressure next step.

Good opening patterns:

- "You open the email expecting a quick reply and instead get a novella of accusations."
- "Parents' evening prep at 10pm and you still have three impossible emails to send."
- "Admin asks for proof you contacted parents. Again."
- "Report comments are always quick until you hit the child you care about most."

Things to avoid:

- Product hype
- Generic productivity talk
- Patronising "teacher hack" language
- Legal or safeguarding advice beyond calm communication guidance

## Reply Templates

Use these when teachers post frustrations on X. Keep replies short. Do not over-explain. Do not hard sell. Aim to be helpful first.

### Angry replies and accusations

1. "I feel this. Those emails can take far more energy than the reply itself. A calm structure usually helps more than a detailed defence."
2. "That sort of message can knock the wind out of you. Short, steady, factual usually lands better than trying to answer every line."
3. "The first draft in your head is rarely the one to send. A pause and a calmer version tomorrow is often worth it."

### Behaviour emails

4. "Behaviour emails are hard because you are trying to be clear without creating a second problem. Factual and brief usually helps."
5. "I think a lot of teachers dread those messages because tone does so much hidden work. Too soft and it says nothing. Too sharp and it escalates."
6. "This is exactly the kind of writing task that drains people after school. Start with the facts, impact, next step, then stop."

### Parent not replying

7. "No reply can be as frustrating as a bad reply, especially when you still need a record of contact. Calm follow-up wording matters."
8. "The follow-up needs to do two jobs at once: keep the relationship workable and give you a clear paper trail."

### Grades and complaints

9. "Grade complaint emails are rarely just about the mark. Usually there is worry sitting underneath it, which makes the wording harder."
10. "You do not need to defend your entire teaching career by email. One calm, clear reply is usually enough."

### Report exhaustion

11. "Report comments are always quick until you hit the child you most want to be fair to. Then every sentence takes ten minutes."
12. "Balanced comments are exhausting because you are trying to be honest and kind in the same line. That is real work, not overthinking."
13. "When report season hits, the hardest comments are usually the ones for pupils who have struggled but still deserve hope in the wording."

### Documentation and admin

14. "Admin asks for proof you contacted parents and suddenly you are rewriting the same thing for the fourth time. That load is very real."
15. "Parent-contact logging is one of those jobs that looks tiny from the outside and quietly eats an entire evening."
